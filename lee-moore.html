02. Coaching philosophy

Lee's approach is built on three principles, drawn from years of working with the same athletes across their development.

One: get them in early. The athletes who go furthest are the ones who started building movement quality and consistency before the growth-related setbacks hit. The earlier the foundations go down, the more durable the senior athlete is.

Two: long-term relationships beat short-term sessions. Lee has worked with individual athletes for nine and ten years at a time. That continuity matters — knowing how an athlete responds to load, what they look like fresh vs. fatigued, when their technique is genuinely off vs. just a bad day. You can't get there in a six-week block.

Three: the gym serves the sport. For competing athletes, the programme is dictated by the playing week. Strength work when the calendar allows, primers when it doesn't, and a willingness to back off when fixture density spikes. S&C exists to make the athlete better on the pitch — not to give the coach impressive numbers on a chart.

03. In Practice: a nine-year case study

In December 2023, Lee wrote a reference for one of his long-term athletes — a young footballer he had been coaching for nine years at that point. What he wrote captures how he thinks about athlete development. The traits he picks out, the language he uses, and what he says matters in a young athlete — it's the philosophy in action.

Reference letter · December 2023
Three words to describe a Proformance athlete
Dedicated
“He is like a sponge. He listens, commits and I only need to show him something once and he's away with it. Hours and hours of hard graft over many years.”
Resilient
“Growth-related issues significantly impacted his training. He never gave up, worked hard week in week out, always giving 100%. I never have to encourage him to work hard because he does it naturally.”
Intelligent
“A clear understanding of the technical aspects of his sport, particularly running mechanics and speed optimisation. The improvements in his speed over the last few years have been ridiculous.”

The athlete had been through growth-related setbacks in his younger years — a period that could easily have derailed a less resilient kid. Lee's view: those setbacks are normal, planned around, and ultimately part of the journey. What matters is consistency, not avoiding the dip.

Nine years on, the athlete is “a robust athlete, capable of withstanding the demanding physical rigors of his sport. He seamlessly navigates the intense training schedules and competitive matches without any setbacks.” That's what long-term athlete development looks like.

“A prime example of the benefit of getting someone in a programme early and he is now excelling.”
Lee Moore, Head Coach · written reference, 1 December 2023
